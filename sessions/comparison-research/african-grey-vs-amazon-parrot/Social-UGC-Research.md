**melissasparrots** (the single most useful passage in the entire SERP):
> "An amazon would LOVE it if you sang to them, danced around, were super dramatic, talked loud… Compare with a typical grey and they might be less than thrilled with loud voices and fast hand movements. A bite from a grey in a similar situation might be more from fear or defensiveness rather than aggression or over excitement. You may have the same end result (bleeding finger) but the motivation and character of the bird that led to that bite will be different… Recently weaned baby greys are often snugly. Recently weaned baby amazons are already wanting to rough house and seriously push the boundaries. A baby amazon can be harder to get along with at 6 months old than it will be at 6 years. With greys, the trend might be reversed a little bit."

And on intelligence, honestly:
> "amazons tend not to be big problem solvers like cockatoos and greys can be… If my amazons can't figure out a foraging toy in short order, they either get bored and give up, or it turns into a wrestling toy… But, I'm not sure being a little less intelligent makes them less desirable pets. Their emotional needs are simpler and easier to meet."

## 7. Instagram & TikTok

**Instagram** — `fancy_feathers_india` reel (May 2025, **469 likes / 17 comments**), ranks on Google page 1 and argues the **opposite** of the consensus:
> "Amazon parrots (Amazona spp.) are often considered superior to African Greys (Psittacus erithacus) in terms of emotional vocalization and sociability… Amazons produce clearer, more expressive speech with natural prosody… In contrast, African Greys can be more sensitive, prone to stress, and socially selective."

We should meet this claim, not duck it: **Greys win vocabulary and context; Amazons win prosody and projection.** That's the honest split, and it's what Avian Avenue's "Greys talk better, Amazons sing better" says in plain English.

## 8. What the UGC corpus tells us to build

1. **Dust is the #1 lived complaint** — repeated in 6 independent sources, and it's what Google's AIO chose to cite. It deserves a real section with the Bird Fancier's Lung entity, not a bullet.
2. **The bite section must be about *behaviour*, not pain.** Amazons telegraph (eye pinning) and bite from excitement/hormones; Greys don't telegraph and bite from fear. Every owner says it; no article does.
3. **Every stereotype in this niche has a credible counter-example**, usually from someone who owns both. A comparison page that presents "typical vs individual" honestly will out-trust every competitor — and it matches Avian Avenue's own conclusion: *"each parrot is an individual within its own species."*
4. **The audience is hostile to buying.** "Adopt don't buy", "you're not ready", "don't get a bird to talk." Any page that ignores this reads as a sales sheet. We must address it directly, and we're allowed to — we're the only ones who can also say *here is the health documentation, here is the weaning policy, here is what happens if you can't keep the bird.*
5. **"There is no guarantee it will talk"** is stated in at least five sources. If our page promises a talking bird, we lose. If our page says it plainly, we're the only breeder that did.
6. **Cost erupts unprompted** ($26,000 vet bill, $20,000 YouTube video) and no article prices anything.
